North American Shared Print Initiative Facilitated by Sustainable Collection Services, a part of OCLC

In 2012, Sustainable Collection Services and the Michican Shared Print Initiative undertook one of the first shared print monographs projects in the US.

Since then, ten other groups have quietly continued this important work with the help of SCS (part of OCLC since 2015) and the GreenGlass Model Builder.

The results speak for themselves: Over 145 academic institutions spanning fourteen U.S. states and five Canadian provinces have joined the OCLC Shared Print Initiative so far.

Those institutions have identified 7.6 million distinct editions for long-term retention, representing 19.7 million print holdings across North America.

Shared print initiatives facilitated by SCS are also starting to take hold across the world, with programs underway in the United Kingdom and Australia.

Shared Print Overview

Models and retention criteria vary according to local and regional priorities, but most of the committed titles are secured under formal Memoranda of Understanding (MOU) for 15 years, often with review every five years. In some respects, these are grass-roots activities, designed to address local needs, but it seems clear that these programs can contribute significantly to a federated national or international solution, such as that envisioned by the MLAâ€™s Working Group on The Future of the Print Record.

Search the Shared Print Collection

What kinds of books are being protected by participating libraries? Most shared print intitatives have focused on guaranteeing the protection of rare titles that are held by very few libraries. This strategy helps secure a robust future for titles that might otherwise be at risk of destruction, as well as gives libraries an opportunity to make effective use of their physical space with the knowledge that the scholarly record will be preserved.

Collaborative Decisions, Made Possible with GreenGlass

The GreenGlass software product has facilitated all shared print initiatives since 2015. GreenGlass employs visualizations and modeling tools to allow groups of libraries to reach consensus quickly, make informed decisions, and fairly assign commitments to retain titles amongst the members of the shared print collection.
